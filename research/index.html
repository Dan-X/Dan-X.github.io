Plasmonic Nanopores

Plasmonic nanopores combine the advantages of nanopore sensing and surface plasmon resonances by introducing confined electromagnetic fields to a solid‐state nanopore. With nanopore sensing, biomolecules can be actively driven towards the nanopore, situated in an insulating membrane, and can be studied through a resistive pulse technique. Despite the advantages of nanopore sensing, it is label-free, fast and high throughput, it also has limitations such as the lack of control of molecule translocation and limited molecular information from the analytes. The plasmonic antennas produce highly localized and enhanced electromagnetic fields, which can be used for label-free molecule sensing, fluorescence and Raman enhancement, and trapping and tweezing single molecules. In this project, we developed different fabrication approches to integrage multiple types of plasmonic antennas on to solid-state nanopores. Such integraged plasmonic nanopore devices shows great advantages and potential applications with regard to label-free single molecule optical sensing and manipulations.

Molecular Plugs on Solid-state Nanopores

Docking of a protein–DNA complex onto a nanopore can provide ample observation time, and has enabled collection of analytic applications of biological nanopores, including DNA sequencing. In this scheme, single protein-DNA molecules are like molecular sized plugs on nanopores, held by the DC electric field across the membrane but unable to translocate because of their relatively larger size comparing to the nanopores. However, the application of the same principle to solid-state nanopores is tempered by poor understanding of the docking process.

In this project, we elucidate the behaviour of individual protein–DNA complexes docked onto a solid-state nanopore by monitoring the nanopore ionic current. Repeat docking of monovalent streptavidin–DNA complexes is found to produce ionic current blockades that fluctuate between discrete levels. We try to discover the roles of the protein plug and the DNA tether in the docking process, finding the docking configurations to determine the multitude of the current blockade levels, whereas the frequency of the current level switching is determined by the interactions between the molecules and the solid-state membrane.
