Current Projects

Plasmonic Nanopores

A plasmonic nanopores is a combination of surface plasmon and a nanopore. ‘Plasmon’ describes the collective electron oscillation in metal excited (usually) by the light. The nanopore, on the other hand, is a nanometer-sized pore in a suspended membrane separating electrolytes with different electric potentials (read more about Surface Plasmons andNanopores). In this project, we make gold nanoantennas at the mouth of nanopores, so the plasmon can be excited right above the entrance of the nanopores and create highly confined and enhanced electromagnetic fields with a size of only several nanometers (called ‘hotspot’).

By doing so, we are aiming to:

  • capture, trap and manipulate single molecules (DNA, protein, etc.) with both electric forces (generated by the DC field across the membrane) and the optical forces (originated from the gradient of EM field/ “the light”);
  • detect the fast translocation of single molecules optically without the need of labelling;
  • acquire chemical structural information of each single molecules by obtaining their molecular spectra which is enhanced by such highly enhanced EM field (such as surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy).

Read more about the Plasmonic Nanopore project on Cees Dekker Lab website.

Past Projects

Molecular Plugs on Solid-state Nanopores

Docking of a protein–DNA complex onto a nanopore can provide ample observation time, and has enabled collection of analytic applications of biological nanopores, including DNA sequencing. In this scheme, single protein-DNA molecules are like molecular sized plugs on nanopores, held by the DC electric field across the membrane but unable to translocate because of their relatively larger size comparing to the nanopores. However, the application of the same principle to solid-state nanopores is tempered by poor understanding of the docking process.

In this project, we elucidate the behaviour of individual protein–DNA complexes docked onto a solid-state nanopore by monitoring the nanopore ionic current. Repeat docking of monovalent streptavidin–DNA complexes is found to produce ionic current blockades that fluctuate between discrete levels. We try to discover the roles of the protein plug and the DNA tether in the docking process, finding the docking configurations to determine the multitude of the current blockade levels, whereas the frequency of the current level switching is determined by the interactions between the molecules and the solid-state membrane.
